A slow quote is a lost job. Research consistently shows that customers make their decision within hours of requesting quotes, not days. A tradesperson who spends two hours manually typing up a quote for a kitchen refit will often lose the work to a competitor who sent a professional PDF within the hour.

This guide covers everything that needs to go into a trade quote, how to price labour and materials correctly, how to handle VAT, and the fastest way to send quotes that look professional and win jobs.

What Every Trade Quote Must Include

A legally sound and professional trade quote in the UK should contain all of the following:

  • Your business name, address, and contact details
  • Customer name and address
  • Date of the quote
  • Quote reference number
  • Detailed description of the work to be carried out
  • Itemised labour costs
  • Itemised materials list with quantities
  • Any exclusions (what the quote does NOT cover)
  • VAT status (included, excluded, or not applicable)
  • Total price
  • Payment terms (deposit, stages, final payment)
  • Quote validity period (typically 30 days)
  • Your signature or authorisation

Missing any of these creates ambiguity that can lead to disputes. "Supply and fit bathroom" is not a quote. "Supply and fit bathroom suite as specified: remove existing suite, install new bath, basin, toilet, and shower enclosure, tile walls to 1.8m, grout and seal, labour 4 days at £320 per day, materials as itemised" is a quote.

The 6-Step Quoting Process

  1. Assess the job thoroughly before pricing

    Visit the site or ask for detailed photos and measurements. Quoting from a phone description alone leads to scope creep. The most common cause of a quote going wrong is underestimating the job because the assessment was rushed.

  2. Calculate your labour time realistically

    Estimate hours for the actual work, plus prep, tidy-up, and any return visits. Then apply your hourly rate. Be honest with yourself: if the job always takes three days, quote for three days, not two.

  3. Price materials at cost plus markup

    List every material item you will need to supply. Price each at your trade cost, then apply a markup of 20 to 35 percent. This markup covers your time sourcing materials, carrying them, and the risk of price changes between quoting and purchasing.

  4. Add overhead and contingency

    For large jobs, add 5 to 10 percent contingency for unexpected complications. For smaller jobs, your hourly rate should already include overhead. Define clearly what will happen if unforeseen work is discovered (for example, rotten joists behind a wall).

  5. Apply VAT if applicable

    If you are VAT-registered, add 20 percent to the total and show it as a separate line. If you are not VAT-registered, state this clearly so the customer does not expect a VAT invoice they can reclaim against.

  6. Send promptly and follow up once

    Send the quote within 24 hours of the assessment. Follow up once, by text or email, 48 to 72 hours later if you have not heard back. More than one follow-up is counterproductive.

How to Price Labour

Labour is where most tradespeople either win or lose margin. The formula is straightforward:

Labour cost = (estimated hours) x (your hourly rate)

The mistake most tradespeople make is using an hourly rate that is too low, or underestimating hours to win the job and then absorbing the loss. Neither is sustainable.

The day rate vs hourly rate decision

For most jobs lasting more than half a day, quote a day rate or a fixed price rather than an hourly figure. Day rates are simpler to understand, reduce customer anxiety about the clock running, and reward your efficiency. Reserve hourly billing for reactive work and jobs where the scope genuinely cannot be defined in advance.

How to Price Materials

Never supply materials at cost. Your time sourcing, transporting, storing, and managing materials has value. The standard approach is:

  • Trade discount materials: price at cost plus 25 to 35 percent
  • Retail-priced materials: price at cost plus 15 to 20 percent
  • Customer-supplied materials: charge a fitting fee only, but note on the quote that you accept no liability for the quality or suitability of customer-supplied materials

Always get accurate quotes from your supplier before including materials in a customer quote, particularly for larger jobs. Material prices move and a quote based on last month's price can erode margin significantly.

Quote vs Estimate: Know the Difference

In UK contract law, a quote is a fixed price offer. Once accepted by the customer, it is legally binding. An estimate is an approximation that can vary.

Always be explicit about which you are providing. Saying "it should cost about £800" and then invoicing £1,200 creates a dispute even if the extra work was genuinely necessary. If the scope is uncertain, provide a written estimate with a defined range, and specify what will trigger a variation.

Common Quoting Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Quoting too low to win the job

A quote that wins a job at a loss is worse than no job. If you are regularly winning every job you quote, your prices are probably too low. A healthy win rate for most tradespeople is 40 to 60 percent of quotes submitted.

Vague scope descriptions

Vague descriptions lead to scope disputes. If you quote "fit new bathroom" and the customer expects you to tile floor to ceiling, you have a problem. Every item that is included should be named. Every item that is excluded should also be named.

No written quote at all

Verbal agreements are hard to enforce. Any job over £200 should have a written quote. Even a WhatsApp message with the scope and price creates a better record than nothing.

Forgetting to add VAT

VAT-registered tradespeople who forget to add VAT to a quote and then invoice with VAT added will either lose the extra margin or face a customer dispute. Always be consistent: quotes and invoices should use the same VAT treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a tradesman quote take to produce?

A professional quote for a standard job should take no more than 15 to 20 minutes to produce manually, or under 2 minutes using an AI quoting tool. The assessment (site visit or photo review) takes as long as it takes, but the document itself should not be a time-consuming task.

Should a tradesman charge for providing a quote?

For standard residential jobs, most UK tradespeople provide free quotes to remain competitive. For large commercial projects, specialist assessments, or jobs requiring survey equipment, a call-out or survey fee is reasonable and common. If you charge for quotes, state this clearly before visiting.

What should a tradesman do if the job costs more than the quote?

Stop work, explain the additional scope to the customer, and get written agreement (even a text message reply) before proceeding. Never complete additional work and then add it to the final invoice without prior agreement. This is the fastest route to a payment dispute.

How long is a tradesman quote valid for?

Standard practice in the UK is 30 days, though this varies by trade. Material prices fluctuate, and a quote for a kitchen fit-out using specific materials should have a shorter validity window than a quote for a day's labour. State the validity period explicitly on every quote.
