UK Tradesman Hourly Rate Guide 2026: What to Charge Per Hour

Updated July 2026 · 10 min read · UK Trades
Quick Answer

UK tradesman hourly rates in 2026 range from £20 to £90 per hour depending on the trade, region, and experience level. Electricians and gas engineers charge the most. Painters and general labourers the least. The average across all trades is approximately £38 per hour.

Undercharging is the single most common financial mistake in the UK trades industry. A tradesperson who charges just £5 per hour below their local market rate, across 1,500 billable hours per year, loses £7,500 in revenue before materials or overhead are even considered.

This guide covers the going rates for every major UK trade, explains what drives regional variation, and shows you how to calculate a rate that actually sustains your business rather than one that just sounds competitive.

Why You Should Not Set Your Rate by Guessing

Most tradespeople set their hourly rate by asking around, looking at what competitors charge, or using whatever they were charging five years ago. None of these methods accounts for your actual costs.

Your hourly rate needs to cover:

  • Your target take-home income after tax and National Insurance
  • Non-billable time: quoting, admin, travel, material collection. The average tradesperson bills only 60 to 70 percent of the hours they work.
  • Tools and equipment: depreciation, replacement, calibration
  • Vehicle costs: fuel, insurance, maintenance, road tax
  • Insurance: public liability, professional indemnity, tools insurance
  • Training and certifications: Gas Safe, NICEIC, CSCS, CPD
  • Software and subscriptions: accounting, quoting, job management
  • Holidays and sick days: self-employed tradespeople have no paid leave
Example: The real cost of a £40/hr electrician in Birmingham

To take home £45,000 per year, an electrician in Birmingham needs to charge roughly £52 to £58 per hour after accounting for 220 working days, 65% billable time, vehicle costs, insurance, and Class 4 National Insurance. A rate of £40/hr results in a take-home closer to £28,000.

Day Rates vs Hourly Rates: Which Should You Use?

Most experienced tradespeople move towards day rates or fixed job prices rather than hourly billing as they grow their business. There are good reasons for this:

  • Day rates reward efficiency. A fast, skilled tradesperson who completes in 6 hours what a slower competitor does in 8 earns less under hourly billing for the same quality of work.
  • Fixed prices reduce disputes. Customers know exactly what they are paying before the job starts. Hourly billing creates anxiety when a job runs long.
  • Day rates are easier to quote remotely. You can give a ballpark price over the phone without committing to an exact hour count.

The practical approach for most tradespeople is to quote fixed prices for standard jobs (a bathroom refit, a consumer unit upgrade, a room repaint) while reverting to a day rate for reactive work, repairs, or jobs where the scope is genuinely unknown until you start.

How to Use Your Hourly Rate to Build Better Quotes

Once you know your sustainable hourly rate, quoting becomes mechanical:

  1. Estimate the hours the job will take (including prep, travel, and tidy-up)
  2. Multiply by your hourly rate for the labour total
  3. Add materials at cost plus your markup (typically 20 to 35%)
  4. Apply VAT if you are VAT-registered
  5. Round to a sensible figure and present as a fixed price

VAT and Hourly Rates

If you are VAT-registered (mandatory above £90,000 annual turnover, optional below), your quoted rate needs to be clear about whether VAT is included or excluded. Most trade quotes present prices excluding VAT and add 20% VAT at the bottom of the invoice.

The practical implication: a £55/hr rate excluding VAT becomes £66/hr for a VAT-registered customer. For domestic customers who cannot reclaim VAT, this increases the effective cost of your work. Always make the VAT status explicit on your quotes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average hourly rate for a tradesman in the UK in 2026?

The average hourly rate across all UK trades in 2026 is approximately £38 per hour. Rates range from around £20 per hour for labourers and handymen up to £90 or more per hour for specialist gas engineers and emergency locksmiths. The national average for electricians is £55 per hour and for plumbers £55 per hour.

Do tradesmen charge more in London?

Yes. London tradesman rates are typically 30 to 40 percent higher than the national average. An electrician charging £55 per hour nationally might charge £70 to £85 per hour in London. This reflects the higher cost of living, parking costs, congestion charges, and travel time within the city.

Should a tradesman charge for travel time?

Yes, in most cases. Travel time is working time for a self-employed tradesperson. The common approach is to include travel within a fixed radius in the base rate, and charge at a reduced rate (often half the hourly rate) for travel beyond that radius. Always make travel charges clear on your quote.

Why do some tradespeople charge less than the going rate?

Usually because they have not calculated their actual costs. A tradesperson charging £35 per hour when the sustainable rate for their trade and region is £50 is effectively subsidising the customer from their own income and savings. The gap between what tradespeople charge and what they need to charge is one of the most common causes of financial difficulty in self-employment.

How often should a tradesperson increase their rates?

At minimum once per year to keep pace with inflation. Material costs, vehicle costs, and insurance premiums all increase annually. A tradesperson who keeps the same rate for three years without review has effectively taken a pay cut every year. The best approach is to review rates each January and communicate any increase to regular clients with at least four weeks notice.
